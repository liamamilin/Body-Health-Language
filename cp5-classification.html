6  Classification

To classify is to divide a field into kinds.

A person may know many separate things and still not yet understand how they are organized. A room may contain tools, books, clothes, and food; a body may contain organs, limbs, and systems; a discussion may involve causes, effects, judgments, and examples. In each case, understanding becomes clearer when language does not merely list the items, but groups them into classes. Classification is one of the first ways thought imposes order on multiplicity.

This is why classification is one of the most basic forms of structure.

After statement, description, definition, and example, expression must begin to organize. Classification is one of the earliest and strongest ways of doing so. It shows that a set of things is not merely many; it is many in an ordered way. Some items belong together because they are of the same kind. Some classes contain smaller subkinds. Some classifications are clear and stable, while others are rough or messy. To classify well is not merely to sort. It is to sort on a usable basis.

6.1 What is classification?

At the level of ordinary language, classification is the act of grouping things into kinds according to some relevant basis.

We notice classification through simple patterns such as:

  • grouping

  • kinds and subkinds

  • the basis of classification

  • the difference between clean and messy categories

Examples include:

  • grouping chairs, tables, and beds as furniture

  • dividing food into fruit, grains, and vegetables

  • separating causes, effects, and conditions in an explanation

  • dividing tasks into urgent and non-urgent

Classification does not merely identify a thing.
It places the thing within an organized field.

6.2 Why classification matters

Classification matters because clear thought often depends on seeing kinds, not just cases.

We need to know:

  • how things are grouped

  • what larger kind a thing belongs to

  • how kinds divide into subkinds

  • what principle is being used to classify

  • when a classification is stable and when it is messy

Without classification, language often remains too flat. A person may mention many items but fail to show how they relate by type. The result is often a list without structure. Classification gives expression a stronger internal order.

6.3 Key distinctions

6.3.1 1. Grouping

The simplest act of classification is grouping similar things together.

Pattern Description Examples
grouping putting similar items into the same class books together, tools together, fruits together
non-grouped listing naming items without showing class structure book, hammer, apple, shoe in one flat list

Examples:

  • Apples, oranges, and bananas can be grouped as fruit.

  • Hammers, knives, and screwdrivers can be grouped as tools.

  • Chairs, tables, and shelves can be grouped as furniture.

  • Walking, running, and jumping can be grouped as forms of movement.

This matters because grouping is one of the simplest ways language reduces disorder.

6.3.2 2. Kinds and subkinds

A class may contain smaller classes within it.

Level Description Examples
kind a broader class animal, fruit, tool, vehicle
subkind a narrower class within that class bird within animal, apple within fruit, knife within tool

Examples:

  • Fruit is a kind; apple is a subkind of fruit.

  • Animal is a kind; bird is a subkind of animal.

  • Vehicle is a kind; bicycle is a subkind of vehicle.

  • Tool is a kind; knife is a subkind of tool.

This matters because classification often has levels. Thought becomes more structured when it can move between them clearly.

6.3.3 3. Basis of classification

A classification is only as good as the basis on which it is made.

Basis Description Examples
function grouping by use or role tools for cutting, tools for measuring
form grouping by physical type round objects, wooden objects
source grouping by origin natural materials, human-made materials
condition grouping by state healthy vs unhealthy, open vs closed
urgency / importance grouping by practical priority urgent tasks, routine tasks

Examples:

  • Books may be classified by subject.

  • Foods may be classified by type.

  • Tasks may be classified by urgency.

  • Roads may be classified by size or traffic level.

This matters because the same set of items can be classified in different ways depending on purpose.

6.3.4 4. Clean vs. messy categories

Not all classifications are equally stable or clean.

Type Description Examples
clean category grouping with relatively clear membership fruits, days of the week, primary colors in a simple teaching context
messy category grouping with overlap, mixed bases, or unclear edges healthy food, useful tools in general, “serious problems” in ordinary speech

Examples:

  • Classifying apples and oranges as fruit is relatively clean.

  • Classifying foods as “good” and “bad” may be much messier.

  • Classifying tasks as “important” may depend on context.

  • Classifying a device as both tool and communication object may create overlap.

This matters because classification is not always perfect. A strong expression often signals whether its categories are stable or rough.

6.4 Core patterns

This chapter rests on a few basic patterns.

6.4.1 Basic expressive patterns

  • Classification groups things into kinds.

  • A kind may contain subkinds.

  • A classification depends on its basis.

  • Some categories are relatively clean.

  • Some categories are messy or overlapping.

  • Structured expression improves when grouping is explicit.

6.4.2 Basic language patterns

  • ___ can be classified as ___.

  • These items belong to the same category.

  • ___ is a kind of ___.

  • ___ is a subkind of ___.

  • The basis of this classification is ___.

  • This category is clear / messy.

  • We can divide ___ into ___ and ___.

6.5 Classification and the movement of this volume

Classification is one of the first major ways expression shows structure.

6.5.1 Part I established the object

  • statement said something

  • description showed it

  • definition bounded it

  • example grounded it

6.5.2 Part II now organizes the field

  • classification groups the object with others of its kind

  • expression begins to show arrangement rather than just isolated clarity

This is a key shift. Language is no longer only saying and clarifying. It is now sorting and structuring.

6.6 Examples

Here are some clean examples of classification in use.

6.6.1 Basic grouping

  • Apples, oranges, and bananas are fruits.

  • Chairs, tables, and beds are furniture.

  • Hammers, knives, and scissors are tools.

  • Walking, running, and jumping are movements.

6.6.2 Kinds and subkinds

  • A bird is a kind of animal.

  • An apple is a kind of fruit.

  • A knife is a kind of tool.

  • A bicycle is a kind of vehicle.

6.6.3 Basis of classification

  • These books are classified by subject.

  • These tools are classified by function.

  • These tasks are classified by urgency.

  • These materials are classified by source.

6.6.4 Clean vs. messy categories

  • Fruit is a relatively clean category in ordinary use.

  • Healthy food is a more context-dependent category.

  • Useful tools depend on the task.

  • Important problems may be grouped differently by different people.

These examples show that classification language often includes:

  • grouping

  • type relation

  • organizing basis

  • level structure

  • category stability

6.7 Why this matters

To understand classification is to understand one of the earliest ways thought becomes visibly organized.

Without classification, expression often becomes a loose list of items or points. A person may mention many things, but fail to show what belongs together. Classification solves this by grouping according to a principle. It makes complexity more manageable.

Classification is also essential because many later structures depend on it:

  • part-whole analysis often begins after kinds are separated

  • comparison becomes clearer when the compared items are classified properly

  • hierarchy builds on classification by layering kinds and subkinds

  • explanation often depends on distinguishing types of cause, condition, or result

  • full thought becomes stronger when categories are stable enough to support later reasoning

Structured expression therefore requires more than saying many things clearly. It requires arranging them into kinds.

6.8 Chapter summary

This chapter introduces classification as one of the most basic ways language organizes structure.

Classification groups things into classes. It distinguishes kinds and subkinds, depends on a basis of grouping, and may produce categories that are either relatively clean or more messy.

The central lessons of this chapter are:

  • classification groups things into kinds

  • kinds may contain subkinds

  • every classification depends on a basis

  • some categories are clean and some are messy

  • clear expression improves when grouping is explicit

  • classification turns a list into an organized field

6.8.1 Core Knowledge (Must Remember)

Core Idea Key Insight Must Remember
Classification Groups things into kinds according to a basis Turns a flat list into an organized field
Kinds and subkinds Classes can contain smaller classes within them “Animal → Bird → Sparrow” shows level structure
Basis of classification The principle used for grouping (function, form, source, etc.) The same set can be classified differently depending on purpose
Clean vs. messy categories Some categories are stable; others have overlap or unclear edges Signal when a category is rough rather than precise
Classification vs. listing Listing names items; classification shows how they relate “Apple, orange, banana” is a list; “These are fruits” is classification

To recognize classification is to strengthen one of the first true structuring powers of language. It allows expression to move beyond isolated cases and begin showing how a domain is divided.
