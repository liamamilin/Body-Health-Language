12  Change

Expression does not only show a process. It must also show the change itself.

A process may unfold through stages, but unless expression marks what is becoming different, the real movement remains unclear. Water heats and then boils. A child grows taller. A wound becomes less painful. A system becomes slower under pressure. A city expands. A person’s condition worsens. In each case, the important point is not only that time passes or that stages occur. It is that something changes.

This is why change matters.

Sequence gives order. Process gives unfolding transformation. Change names the difference that emerges through that unfolding. It allows language to say not only that there was movement, but what kind of movement it was: becoming, increasing, decreasing, growing, declining, stabilizing, or becoming unstable. Structured expression becomes much stronger when it can point directly to the change rather than leaving it hidden inside the process.

12.1 What is change?

At the level of ordinary language, change is the movement from one state, level, or condition to another.

We notice change through simple patterns such as:

  • becoming

  • increasing and decreasing

  • growth and decline

  • stable and unstable change

Examples include:

  • becoming tired

  • becoming clear

  • increasing pressure

  • decreasing temperature

  • growth in skill

  • decline in health

A process may contain many steps.
Change tells us what is actually becoming different.

12.2 Why change matters

Change matters because many important expressions depend on identifying the direction and character of transformation.

We need to know:

  • what is becoming different

  • in what direction the change moves

  • whether something is increasing or decreasing

  • whether the change is growth, decline, or some other shift

  • whether the change is stable or unstable

Without this, language may describe motion without showing development. A person may list events, mention stages, and still fail to state what actually changed. Change gives dynamic expression a clearer center.

12.3 Key distinctions

12.3.1 1. Becoming

One of the most basic ways to express change is through becoming.

Pattern Description Examples
becoming moving from one condition into another becoming tired, becoming warm, becoming quiet
fixed state remaining in a condition without emphasizing transition being tired, being warm, being quiet

Examples:

  • The room became quiet.

  • He became tired after the walk.

  • The sky became darker.

  • The problem became clearer after discussion.

This matters because becoming marks transition directly. It shifts expression from static condition to change in condition.

12.3.2 2. Increasing and decreasing

Many changes can be expressed as movement along a scale.

Direction Description Examples
increasing moving upward in amount, intensity, or level increasing speed, increasing pressure, increasing cost
decreasing moving downward in amount, intensity, or level decreasing noise, decreasing energy, decreasing temperature

Examples:

  • The noise increased.

  • Her confidence increased over time.

  • The pain decreased after rest.

  • The temperature dropped during the night.

This matters because some of the clearest changes are scalar rather than categorical.

12.3.3 3. Growth and decline

Some changes are best understood as development upward or downward across time.

Pattern Description Examples
growth increase in size, strength, capacity, or development growth in skill, growth in trust, growth in population
decline weakening, reduction, or deterioration decline in health, decline in quality, decline in attention

Examples:

  • The child showed steady growth.

  • His skill improved through practice.

  • The old building declined over time.

  • Her health went into decline after the illness.

This matters because growth and decline add evaluative or developmental direction to change.

12.3.4 4. Stable vs. unstable change

Not all change has the same character. Some changes settle into a new condition, while others remain unstable or fluctuating.

Type Description Examples
stable change change that settles into a relatively steady result recovery after treatment, steady improvement, fixed adjustment
unstable change change that remains uneven, fragile, or fluctuating unstable recovery, changing mood, irregular performance

Examples:

  • His condition improved and then stabilized.

  • The system became more efficient over time.

  • Her mood changed quickly and remained unstable.

  • The process produced unstable results.

This matters because the quality of change often matters as much as the fact of change.

12.4 Core patterns

This chapter rests on a few basic patterns.

12.4.1 Basic expressive patterns

  • Change marks what becomes different.

  • Becoming is a direct form of change.

  • Some changes are increases.

  • Some changes are decreases.

  • Some changes are growth.

  • Some changes are decline.

  • Some changes are stable and others unstable.

12.4.2 Basic language patterns

  • ___ became ___.

  • ___ increased.

  • ___ decreased.

  • There was growth in ___.

  • There was decline in ___.

  • The change was stable.

  • The change remained unstable.

  • Over time, ___ changed into ___.

12.5 Change and the movement of this volume

Change sharpens the process-oriented expression introduced in the last chapter.

12.5.1 Sequence showed ordered steps

  • first

  • next

  • then

  • finally

12.5.2 Process showed unfolding transformation

  • stages

  • input to output

  • connected movement

12.5.3 Change now identifies the transformation itself

  • what became different

  • in what direction

  • with what kind of development

  • with what degree of stability

This is an important refinement. Language is no longer only showing that something unfolds. It is specifying the nature of that unfolding.

12.6 Examples

Here are some clean examples of change language.

12.6.1 Becoming

  • The room became quiet.

  • The water became hot.

  • He became tired after the trip.

  • The idea became clearer with discussion.

12.6.2 Increasing / decreasing

  • The cost increased this year.

  • The noise slowly decreased.

  • Her confidence increased through practice.

  • The pressure dropped after the valve was opened.

12.6.3 Growth / decline

  • The child showed healthy growth.

  • Trust grew between them over time.

  • The old system declined in reliability.

  • His attention declined by the afternoon.

12.6.4 Stable vs. unstable change

  • Her recovery was slow but stable.

  • The machine became more reliable.

  • His condition changed in unstable ways.

  • The results improved at first, then became uneven.

These examples show that change language often includes:

  • transition

  • direction

  • increase or decrease

  • development

  • stability or instability

12.7 Why this matters

To understand change is to understand that dynamic expression requires more than order and process. It requires explicit recognition of transformation.

Without this idea, language may show that something happened but fail to say what actually shifted. A person may describe a process and still leave the real development hidden. Change solves this by naming the movement itself.

Change is also essential because many later structures depend on it:

  • development is a larger, more organized form of change

  • explanation often depends on identifying what changed and why

  • evaluation often depends on whether change is good, bad, stable, or unstable

  • full thought becomes stronger when transformation is explicitly marked

  • dynamic reasoning improves when direction and stability are visible

Structured expression therefore requires more than process. It requires the ability to state what became different.

12.8 Chapter summary

This chapter introduces change as the explicit marking of transformation within dynamic expression.

Expression does not only present process. It must also identify the change itself. Important ideas include becoming, increasing and decreasing, growth and decline, and stable versus unstable change.

The central lessons of this chapter are:

  • change marks what becomes different

  • becoming is a direct expression of change

  • some changes are increases and some are decreases

  • some changes are growth and some are decline

  • stable and unstable change differ in character

  • clear dynamic expression depends on identifying change directly

12.8.1 Core Knowledge (Must Remember)

Core Idea Key Insight Must Remember
Change Marks what becomes different across time Not just that time passes, but what actually shifts
Becoming Directly marks transition from one state to another “The room became quiet” — the simplest expression of change
Increasing & decreasing Movement along a scale (more/less, stronger/weaker) “Noise increased; temperature decreased” — scalar changes are clearest
Growth & decline Directional development over time Growth in skill; decline in health — adds evaluative direction
Stable vs. unstable change Some changes settle; others remain fluctuating The quality of change matters as much as the fact of change
Change vs. process Process shows unfolding; change names the transformation itself “The water boiled” (change) vs. “First heat, then bubbles, then steam” (process)

To recognize change is to move from the unfolding of process toward the explicit language of transformation. This is one of the key ways expression becomes able to track reality in motion.
