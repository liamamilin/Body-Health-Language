Appendix A: Core Vocabulary of Body & Health Language

This appendix provides a comprehensive glossary of the core vocabulary used throughout Body & Health Language. Each term includes a short definition, its body-health use, and an example phrase. Terms are organized by conceptual category.

Core Body-Health Concepts

body — The living biological system that includes the nervous system, musculoskeletal system, metabolic system, cardiovascular system, immune system, and all physiological systems that sustain life and enable action. In this book, body is not opposed to mind; the body is the infrastructure of mind. Use: Identify the body as the foundation of attention, emotion, decision, and action. Example: “My body is the infrastructure through which I learn, work, and transform.”

health — The stability of function under real life demands. Not merely the absence of diagnosed disease. A person may have no diagnosable condition yet be unable to sleep, sustain attention, regulate emotion, or recover from normal daily load. Use: Evaluate health by functional capacity, not by diagnosis alone. Example: “My health is stable if I can sleep reliably, sustain attention, and recover from daily load.”

function — What the body can do: sleep, breathe, move, digest, regulate temperature, maintain energy, sustain attention, recover from effort. The operational dimension of health. Use: Assess function rather than only symptom absence. Example: “My sleep function is reduced when I cannot fall asleep within thirty minutes.”

stability — The ability to maintain function across varying conditions. A stable body can handle moderate stress without collapsing, recover from fatigue, maintain attention across a work session, and regulate emotion under pressure. Use: Track whether function holds steady across different days and contexts. Example: “My energy stability is good; I maintain consistent focus from morning through early afternoon.”

capacity — The total available energy, attention, and resilience that a person can draw on for action. Capacity fluctuates with sleep, stress, nutrition, movement, recovery, and load. Managing capacity is the core skill of sustainable productivity. Use: Assess capacity before committing to action. Example: “My current capacity is low; I should not take on additional cognitive load today.”

baseline — The typical resting level of energy, mood, attention, and body function for a given person under normal conditions. Baseline is the reference point for detecting deviation. Use: Know your baseline to recognize when something is off. Example: “My baseline energy is moderate; if I feel extreme fatigue, something has shifted.”

resilience — The ability to recover from stress, load, and disruption. Resilience is not the absence of stress but the capacity to return to baseline after stress. Use: Build resilience through recovery, not through constant exposure. Example: “My resilience is higher when I sleep well and maintain regular movement.”

vulnerability — The state of being more susceptible to stress, overload, or health disruption due to low resilience, depleted capacity, or ongoing load. Vulnerability is not weakness; it is information about current system state. Use: Identify vulnerability before it becomes breakdown. Example: “After a week of poor sleep, I am more vulnerable to stress and should reduce load.”

capacity reserve — The unused portion of total capacity available for unexpected demands. Capacity reserve is the buffer between normal function and breakdown. Use: Maintain reserve for emergencies, not exhaust capacity daily. Example: “I have no capacity reserve left; even a small extra demand will overload me.”

embodied productivity — The recognition that sustainable productivity depends on body state, not only on time management or task organization. Embodied productivity manages energy, recovery, and capacity alongside tasks. Use: Design productivity systems around body capacity, not against it. Example: “Embodied productivity means I schedule recovery before I need it.”

action readiness — The state of being prepared to act, combining available energy, appropriate arousal, and clear intention. Action readiness is not the same as motivation; it depends on body state. Use: Check action readiness before attributing delay to motivation. Example: “I feel intention but my body is not ready to act; I may need to regulate or rest first.”

Body Signals and Sensations

signal — Any information the body produces: a sensation, a change in energy, a shift in mood, a tension, a discomfort, a pain, a change in appetite, sleep, or attention. A signal is raw data before interpretation. Use: Notice the signal before assigning meaning. Example: “My body signal is a dull ache behind my eyes.”

symptom — A signal that has been interpreted as indicating a possible health issue. The same physical experience can be a signal in one context and a symptom in another. Use: Distinguish between a raw signal and a concern-labeled symptom. Example: “The headache became a symptom when I started worrying about what it means.”

sensation — The raw experience of a body signal before interpretation. Sensation is what you notice when you direct attention to a part of your body: warmth, coolness, pressure, tingling, tightness, looseness, heaviness, lightness. Use: Describe the sensation without adding interpretation. Example: “The sensation is a tight band of pressure around my forehead.”

discomfort — An unpleasant or bothersome sensation that is not necessarily dangerous. Discomfort exists on a spectrum from mild unease to significant bother. Use: Acknowledge discomfort without escalating it to danger. Example: “The discomfort in my lower back is noticeable but not severe.”

pain — An unpleasant sensory and emotional experience associated with actual or potential tissue damage. Pain is both a sensation and an interpretation; it demands attention and assessment. Use: Distinguish pain from general discomfort; assess intensity, location, and quality. Example: “The pain is sharp, localized to my right shoulder, and does not subside with rest.”

observation — The practice of noticing body signals and states without immediate interpretation or reaction. Observation is the foundation of awareness. Use: Observe before deciding whether to act. Example: “I observe that my breathing is shallow and my shoulders are raised; I will observe for another minute before adjusting.”

Interpretation and Meaning

interpretation — The meaning assigned to a sensation or signal. Interpretation can be accurate, exaggerated, minimized, or completely wrong. Separating sensation from interpretation is a core skill of body-health language. Use: Separate “what I feel” from “what I think it means.” Example: “The interpretation I am making is that this headache means I am dehydrated.”

body narrative — The ongoing story a person tells about their body, health, and capacity. A body narrative can be accurate, catastrophizing, dismissive, or constructive. Use: Examine your body narrative critically. Example: “My body narrative tends toward catastrophizing: every new sensation feels like a serious problem.”

catastrophizing — The cognitive pattern of interpreting a body signal as far worse than the evidence supports. Catastrophizing amplifies distress and can lead to unnecessary avoidance or medical seeking. Use: Recognize catastrophizing and return to sensation-level description. Example: “I notice I am catastrophizing: this mild chest tightness is probably anxiety, not a heart problem.”

Pattern and Context

pattern — The recurrence of a signal across time, context, and conditions. A single headache is an event; headaches that appear every time you skip breakfast are a pattern. Use: Look for patterns before making health decisions. Example: “The pattern is that my scalp itch appears during high-focus work and subsides when I move.”

trigger — A specific stimulus, condition, or event that precedes and may contribute to a body signal or state change. Triggers are not necessarily causes; they are correlates that merit investigation. Use: Identify triggers to anticipate and manage body responses. Example: “The trigger for my tension headache seems to be three or more hours of continuous screen work.”

context — The surrounding conditions in which a body signal appears: time of day, activity, environment, emotional state, recent sleep, recent meals, recent stress. Context makes a signal interpretable. Use: Always place a signal in its context. Example: “In the context of poor sleep and high deadline pressure, this fatigue is expected.”

frequency — How often a body signal or event occurs within a given time period (e.g., daily, weekly, monthly). Frequency helps distinguish isolated events from patterns. Use: Track frequency to assess whether a signal is becoming more common. Example: “The frequency of my headaches has increased from once a week to four times a week.”

duration — How long a body signal or event lasts when it occurs. Duration helps distinguish transient fluctuations from persistent issues. Use: Note duration to assess significance. Example: “The dizziness lasts about thirty seconds and then resolves completely.”

intensity — The strength or severity of a body signal, often rated on a scale. Intensity helps distinguish mild, moderate, and severe signals. Use: Rate intensity to track changes over time. Example: “The pain intensity is a 4 out of 10, noticeable but not disabling.”

correlation — A relationship between two variables (e.g., a trigger and a signal) where changes in one are associated with changes in the other. Correlation is not causation but is useful for pattern detection. Use: Note correlations to generate hypotheses about triggers. Example: “There is a strong correlation between my headache frequency and the number of hours I spend in meetings.”

Stress, Arousal, and Nervous System

stress — A body state of activation in response to demand. Stress is not inherently bad; moderate stress sharpens attention and mobilizes energy. Chronic or excessive stress – stress that outlasts the demand or exceeds the body’s capacity to recover – becomes harmful. Use: Distinguish useful stress from harmful accumulated stress. Example: “Moderate stress helps me focus, but after four hours of intense work, the stress is no longer productive.”

arousal — The level of nervous system activation, ranging from low (drowsy, sluggish, disengaged) through moderate (alert, focused, engaged) to high (agitated, anxious, overstimulated). Arousal is the dimension that regulation targets. Use: Assess arousal level before choosing a regulation strategy. Example: “My arousal is too high for focused work; I need to down-regulate before continuing.”

activation — The mobilization of physiological resources for action. Activation includes increased heart rate, muscle readiness, and alertness. Activation is part of the stress response. Use: Recognize activation as a normal response, not necessarily a problem. Example: “My body is activated for the presentation; that is appropriate and helpful.”

nervous system — The body’s network of nerves and neural structures that processes sensory information, controls movement, regulates internal organs, and coordinates stress and recovery responses. In this book, the nervous system is the primary lens for understanding regulation. Use: Consider nervous system state as a factor in every body signal. Example: “My nervous system is currently in a sympathetic-dominant state; I need to activate the parasympathetic response.”

sympathetic activation — The branch of the autonomic nervous system associated with the fight-or-flight response. Sympathetic activation increases heart rate, breathing, and alertness while redirecting resources from digestion and repair. Use: Recognize sympathetic activation as a normal but temporary state. Example: “My sympathetic activation is high; I notice rapid heartbeat and shallow breathing.”

calm — A state of low sympathetic activation and balanced autonomic function. Calm is the baseline for effective regulation, clear thinking, and restorative rest. Use: Aim for calm as the default state between demands. Example: “I need to return to calm before I can evaluate this situation clearly.”

alertness — A state of moderate arousal with focused attention and readiness to respond. Alertness is distinct from anxiety; it is activation without distress. Use: Cultivate alertness without slipping into overactivation. Example: “I want to maintain alertness for this task without crossing into overactivation.”

overactivation — A state of excessive arousal where the body is mobilized beyond what the situation requires. Overactivation impairs clear thinking, restful sleep, and accurate interpretation of body signals. Use: Recognize overactivation early and intervene. Example: “I am overactivated; my heart is racing, my mind is spinning, and I cannot focus on any single task.”

Emotion and Somatic Response

emotion — A complex state involving subjective feeling, physiological response, cognitive appraisal, and behavioral tendency. In this book, emotion is examined through its body manifestations. Use: Read emotion through body signals. Example: “I recognize the emotion is anxiety because my chest feels tight and my stomach is unsettled.”

tension — Sustained contraction of muscles, often in the shoulders, neck, jaw, back, or face. Tension is both a response to stress and a perpetuator of stress. Use: Scan for tension as an early sign of accumulated stress. Example: “I notice tension in my jaw and shoulders; this suggests I am carrying stress from the work session.”

somatic response — Any body reaction to a stimulus, emotion, or thought. Somatic responses include changes in breathing, heart rate, muscle tension, skin temperature, digestion, and posture. Use: Observe somatic responses as data, not as problems. Example: “My somatic response to the deadline is shallow breathing and a churning stomach.”

muscle tone — The baseline level of tension in muscles at rest. Healthy muscle tone supports posture and movement; excessive tone contributes to stiffness and discomfort. Use: Assess muscle tone to gauge overall tension level. Example: “My muscle tone feels elevated; my shoulders do not relax even when I try to lower them.”

breath — The process of inhaling and exhaling. Breath is both an automatic function and a voluntary regulation tool. Breath patterns reflect and influence nervous system state. Use: Use breath as a regulation tool and a signal source. Example: “My breath is shallow and fast; I will lengthen my exhales to activate the calming response.”

posture — The position of the body in space, especially the alignment of spine, head, shoulders, and hips. Posture affects breathing, muscle tension, pain, and energy. Use: Check posture as both a signal source and a modifiable factor. Example: “My posture is slumped forward; this may be contributing to my tension headache.”

impulse — A sudden urge to act, often arising from body state before conscious decision. Impulses include the urge to scratch, move, eat, scroll, or escape. Use: Observe impulses without necessarily acting on them. Example: “I feel an impulse to check my phone; this may be a sign of overactivation, not a real need.”

Self-Regulation

anxiety — A state of heightened arousal, worry, and vigilance, often accompanied by body symptoms such as chest tightness, shallow breathing, stomach discomfort, restlessness, and tension. Anxiety is not merely a mental state; it is a body state. Use: Address anxiety through body regulation, not only through reasoning. Example: “I cannot think my way out of this anxiety; I need to regulate my body first.”

self-regulation — The intentional modification of arousal and body state toward a target range. Self-regulation includes down-regulation (calming, slowing, relaxing) and up-regulation (energizing, activating, mobilizing). Use: Practice regulation as a skill, not a fix. Example: “I need self-regulation, not more pressure; my nervous system is overactivated.”

grounding — A regulation practice that connects attention to the present moment and the physical body, often through breath, sensation, or environmental awareness. Grounding reduces dissociation and overactivation. Use: Use grounding when feeling overwhelmed or disconnected. Example: “I will ground myself by feeling my feet on the floor and taking three slow breaths.”

downshift — A deliberate reduction in arousal from high to moderate or low. Downshift may involve slowing breathing, reducing stimulation, changing environment, or resting. Use: Downshift when overactivation impairs function. Example: “I need to downshift before the next meeting; I will walk away from my desk and breathe slowly for two minutes.”

pause — A deliberate stop in action before responding to a signal, impulse, or demand. The pause creates space between stimulus and response. Use: Insert a pause before deciding what a signal means or how to respond. Example: “Before scratching, I will pause and observe the sensation for ten seconds.”

recovery action — A specific behavior performed to restore body state toward baseline (e.g., slow breathing, stretching, drinking water, walking, lying down, closing eyes). Recovery actions are distinct from distractions. Use: Choose recovery actions that genuinely restore, not just distract. Example: “Stepping outside for fresh air is a recovery action; scrolling social media is a distraction.”

Sleep, Rest, and Recovery

sleep — The natural, recurring state of reduced consciousness and metabolic activity during which the body and brain restore function. Sleep is the primary recovery process for the nervous system, immune system, and metabolic system. Use: Prioritize sleep as the foundation of recovery. Example: “My sleep duration is adequate but my sleep quality is poor; I wake frequently during the night.”

rest — Any period of reduced activity during which the body and mind are not engaged in demanding tasks. Rest is not the absence of work; it is the condition for restoration. Use: Distinguish genuine rest from passive stimulation. Example: “Lying down without a screen is rest; lying down while scrolling is not full rest.”

restoration — The process of returning body systems to baseline function after load. Restoration occurs during sleep, rest, and recovery actions. Use: Assess whether true restoration has occurred. Example: “After a full night of sleep, I should feel some restoration; if I do not, something is interfering with sleep quality.”

sleep quality — The depth, continuity, and restorative value of sleep. Sleep quality is distinct from sleep duration. A person can sleep eight hours yet have poor quality due to interruptions, shallow sleep, or insufficient deep sleep. Use: Evaluate both duration and quality. Example: “My sleep quality is low; I wake up tired even after seven hours in bed.”

rest debt — The accumulated gap between the rest the body needs and the rest it has received. Rest debt builds when sleep is insufficient, quality is poor, or recovery is inadequate. Use: Monitor rest debt and plan to repay it. Example: “After three days of short sleep, I have a rest debt that will require intentional recovery.”

deep rest — A state of profound relaxation during which the nervous system shifts into parasympathetic dominance and the body enters repair mode. Deep rest is more restorative than passive rest. Use: Seek practices that produce deep rest, not just time off. Example: “A twenty-minute nap or a period of slow, conscious breathing can produce deep rest.”

passive rest — Rest that occurs without active intervention, such as lying down, sitting quietly, or staring out a window. Passive rest allows some recovery but may not fully restore depleted capacity. Use: Combine passive rest with active recovery for optimal restoration. Example: “Passive rest helps me feel slightly better, but I also need active recovery to restore full capacity.”

active recovery — Intentional, low-intensity activity that promotes restoration (e.g., gentle walking, stretching, foam rolling, light movement). Active recovery maintains circulation and mobility while the body rests. Use: Use active recovery between intense work periods. Example: “A ten-minute walk after a long writing session is active recovery for my back and eyes.”

Load, Fatigue, and Capacity

fatigue — A state of reduced physical or mental capacity resulting from prolonged activity, insufficient recovery, or accumulated load. Fatigue is normal and serves as a signal that recovery is needed. Use: Distinguish normal fatigue from overload. Example: “This fatigue feels normal; it responds to rest and does not persist into the next day.”

load — The total demand placed on the body and mind. Load includes cognitive load (learning, problem-solving, decision-making), emotional load (stress, uncertainty, interpersonal demands), physical load (sedentary posture, movement, exercise), and metabolic load (digestion, temperature regulation, immune response). Use: Assess total load, not just task load. Example: “My total load is high this week: intense cognitive work, emotional stress from the application process, and poor sleep.”

overload — The condition where demand consistently exceeds recovery and capacity. Overload produces cumulative fatigue, declining attention, emotional instability, reduced immune function, and increased risk of burnout and illness. Use: Identify overload before it becomes burnout. Example: “I am in overload: my fatigue does not resolve with rest, my attention is declining, and I feel emotionally brittle.”

depletion — The state of having used most or all available capacity reserves. Depletion is characterized by low energy, reduced motivation, difficulty concentrating, and increased vulnerability to stress. Use: Recognize depletion as a signal to stop, not push through. Example: “I am depleted; continuing to work will only deepen the recovery deficit.”

capacity mismatch — A condition in which the demands placed on the body (load) exceed the body’s available resources (capacity). The mismatch can be acute (a single day of excessive demand) or chronic (weeks of demand exceeding recovery). Use: Detect mismatch early and adjust load or increase recovery. Example: “There is a capacity mismatch: my learning load is high, but my sleep and recovery are insufficient.”

recovery deficit — The accumulated gap between recovery received and recovery needed. Recovery deficit grows when recovery actions are insufficient relative to load. Use: Track recovery deficit as an early warning of overload. Example: “My recovery deficit is growing; I need to prioritize rest over additional work this weekend.”

burnout risk — The likelihood of entering a state of physical, emotional, and mental exhaustion caused by prolonged overload and insufficient recovery. Burnout risk increases when recovery deficit persists for weeks or months. Use: Monitor burnout risk indicators: persistent fatigue, emotional numbness, reduced performance, and loss of engagement. Example: “I am at burnout risk: I have felt exhausted for three weeks, and I no longer feel satisfaction from my work.”

Rhythm and Sustainability

rhythm — A recurring pattern of activity and rest that structures daily, weekly, or seasonal life. Rhythm is the temporal architecture of sustainable capacity management. Use: Design rhythms, not just schedules. Example: “My daily rhythm includes focused work in the morning, lighter tasks after lunch, and movement in the late afternoon.”

cycle — A complete iteration of a recurring pattern, such as a work-rest cycle or a recovery cycle. Cycles can be daily, weekly, or seasonal. Use: Observe cycles to understand your natural energy fluctuations. Example: “My energy cycle peaks in the late morning and dips in the early afternoon.”

recovery rhythm — The deliberate structuring of recovery intervals within and between work periods. Recovery rhythm includes micro-breaks, daily rest, weekly recovery days, and seasonal breaks. Use: Design recovery rhythm before you need it. Example: “My recovery rhythm includes a five-minute break every hour, a thirty-minute lunch away from screens, and one full rest day per week.”

work-rest ratio — The proportion of time spent in active work versus time spent in recovery. An optimal ratio depends on the person, the task, and the context. Use: Monitor and adjust your work-rest ratio. Example: “My current work-rest ratio is unbalanced: I work for four hours without a break and then collapse.”

sustainability — The capacity to maintain a given level of activity or performance over the long term without breakdown. Sustainability requires that recovery at least equals load over time. Use: Evaluate sustainability, not just short-term output. Example: “This pace is not sustainable; I can maintain it for two weeks but not for two months.”

pacing — The deliberate management of effort distribution across time to avoid overload and maintain consistent output. Pacing is the opposite of pushing hard then crashing. Use: Practice pacing as a long-term strategy. Example: “I am pacing myself: I work at 80% intensity rather than 100%, so I can sustain effort all week.”

load management — The active process of monitoring, adjusting, and balancing load across domains (cognitive, emotional, physical, metabolic) to stay within capacity. Load management is the operational arm of sustainability. Use: Practice load management daily. Example: “I am managing my load by reducing meeting time this week because my cognitive load from learning is already high.”

Movement and Physical Conditioning

movement — Any change in body position or activity, from small adjustments to full exercise. Movement is distinct from formal exercise; it includes walking, stretching, shifting posture, and daily activity. Use: Prioritize regular movement, not only formal workouts. Example: “My movement today consisted of walking to the kitchen and back; I need more variety.”

exercise — Planned, structured, and repetitive physical activity performed to improve or maintain physical fitness. Exercise is a subset of movement. Use: Incorporate exercise as part of a complete movement practice. Example: “My exercise routine includes strength training twice per week and cardiovascular work once per week.”

strength — The ability of muscles to generate force. Strength supports posture, movement, injury prevention, and metabolic health. Use: Build strength for functional capacity, not appearance. Example: “My strength is sufficient for daily activities but insufficient for sustained standing work.”

mobility — The ability to move joints and muscles through their full range of motion without restriction or pain. Mobility is distinct from flexibility; it includes strength through range. Use: Maintain mobility to prevent stiffness and injury. Example: “My hip mobility is limited; sitting for long hours has reduced my range of motion.”

sedentary load — The physiological burden of prolonged sitting or inactivity. Sedentary load affects the neck, shoulders, back, hips, eyes, circulation, mood, and energy. Use: Recognize sedentary load as a real form of physical demand. Example: “After six hours of writing, my sedentary load is significant; I need movement to offset it.”

physical conditioning — The general state of cardiovascular, muscular, and metabolic fitness. Conditioning determines how efficiently the body handles physical demand. Use: Maintain basic conditioning for overall capacity. Example: “My physical conditioning is moderate; I can walk for an hour but cannot run for ten minutes without being winded.”

Nutrition and Hydration

nutrition — The intake of food and its impact on body function. Nutrition provides fuel, structure, and regulatory compounds for all body systems. Use: Think of nutrition as fuel and support, not as moral discipline. Example: “My nutrition today was inconsistent: I skipped breakfast and ate a heavy lunch, causing an afternoon energy crash.”

hydration — The state of having adequate water in the body for optimal physiological function. Dehydration affects energy, attention, mood, and physical performance. Use: Monitor hydration as a basic support factor. Example: “I am mildly dehydrated; my urine is dark and my attention is flagging.”

fuel — The energy substrates (primarily glucose and fatty acids) that the body uses for cellular function, muscle contraction, and brain activity. Fuel is a neutral term; it describes what the body runs on. Use: Assess whether the body has adequate fuel for current demands. Example: “I have not eaten in six hours; my fuel supply is low, and my energy is dropping.”

blood sugar stability — The maintenance of glucose levels within a functional range, avoiding sharp spikes and crashes. Blood sugar instability can cause energy swings, mood changes, and attention problems. Use: Eat in ways that support stable blood sugar. Example: “My blood sugar is unstable; I feel energetic after meals but crash within two hours.”

physical support — The basic nutritional and hydration practices that enable the body to function: regular meals, adequate water, balanced macronutrients, and sufficient micronutrients. Use: Ensure physical support is in place before considering optimization. Example: “I need to address basic physical support before adding any supplements or special diets.”

meal rhythm — The timing and regularity of meals. A stable meal rhythm supports blood sugar stability, energy consistency, and digestive function. Use: Establish a meal rhythm that fits your daily schedule. Example: “My meal rhythm is erratic; I eat whenever I remember, which causes energy fluctuations.”

body resource — The stored and available nutrients, energy, and hydration the body can draw on. Body resource is the internal supply that supports function between meals and during high demand. Use: Manage body resources consciously. Example: “After a week of poor eating, my body resources are depleted; I feel weak and my recovery is slow.”

Routine, Habit, and Environment

routine — A sequence of actions performed regularly, often at specific times. Routines reduce decision fatigue and create stable conditions for body function. Use: Design routines that support health, not only productivity. Example: “My morning routine includes drinking water, stretching, and reviewing my plan for the day.”

habit — An automatic or semi-automatic behavior triggered by a cue in a consistent context. Habits are the building blocks of routine. Use: Shape habits through environment design, not willpower. Example: “I have developed the habit of drinking water immediately after waking.”

environment — The physical, social, and informational surroundings in which a person lives and works. The environment shapes behavior through cues, friction, and defaults. Use: Design environment to support desired health behaviors. Example: “My environment is not supporting my health: snacks are visible, water is not nearby, and my chair is uncomfortable.”

cue — A stimulus that triggers a behavior or habit. Cues can be environmental (time of day, location, object), internal (sensation, emotion), or social (other people’s behavior). Use: Identify and adjust cues that trigger unwanted or wanted behaviors. Example: “The cue for my snacking is the bowl of candy on my desk; removing it eliminates the cue.”

friction — The effort or difficulty required to perform an action. High friction reduces the likelihood of a behavior; low friction increases it. Use: Reduce friction for desired behaviors; increase friction for undesired ones. Example: “I added friction to phone checking by keeping my phone in another room during work hours.”

default — The option that occurs without active decision. Defaults powerfully shape behavior because they require no effort. Use: Set health-supporting defaults. Example: “My default lunch is now a balanced meal because I prepare it the night before.”

health system — The integrated set of routines, habits, environments, and practices that maintain body function and prevent breakdown. A health system is not a single practice but a designed infrastructure. Use: Design a health system, not just isolated habits. Example: “My health system includes a sleep routine, a movement practice, a nutrition plan, and a weekly review.”

maintenance design — The intentional structuring of maintenance activities (movement, nutrition, sleep, environment) to require minimal daily decision-making. Good maintenance design makes health practices the easy path. Use: Apply maintenance design to reduce reliance on willpower. Example: “My maintenance design ensures that water, healthy food, and movement opportunities are always available.”

Health Boundaries and Medical Attention

health boundary — The line between self-care and professional medical attention. Knowing where this boundary lies is essential for responsible health management. Use: Establish clear criteria for when to seek help. Example: “My health boundary is: if a symptom persists beyond two weeks or worsens, I will consult a doctor.”

self-care — The actions a person takes to maintain or improve their own health, including rest, movement, nutrition, hygiene, and stress management. Self-care is distinct from self-diagnosis or self-treatment. Use: Practice self-care for maintenance; seek professional care for persistent issues. Example: “My self-care practice includes daily stretching, adequate hydration, and consistent sleep hours.”

medical attention — Consultation with a licensed healthcare professional (doctor, nurse, therapist, specialist) for evaluation, diagnosis, or treatment of a health concern. Use: Seek medical attention when signals persist, worsen, or impair function. Example: “I will seek medical attention because this pain has persisted for two weeks and is not improving.”

risk — The probability that a body signal or condition may lead to harm or deterioration. Risk assessment considers duration, intensity, persistence, worsening, and functional impact. Use: Assess risk as part of health boundary decisions. Example: “The risk of ignoring this symptom is moderate because it impairs my ability to sleep.”

red flag — A signal or symptom that suggests the possibility of a serious condition requiring prompt medical evaluation. Red flags include sudden severe pain, chest pain, shortness of breath, neurological changes, bleeding, high fever, and rapid unexplained weight loss. Use: Know red flags and act on them without delay. Example: “Sudden chest pain with shortness of breath is a red flag; I will seek emergency care.”

persistence — The quality of a signal continuing over time without resolution. Persistence is a key factor in determining whether a signal requires professional evaluation. Use: Track persistence as a decision criterion. Example: “This cough has persisted for three weeks with no improvement; it is time to see a doctor.”

worsening — The pattern of a signal increasing in intensity, frequency, or duration over time. Worsening signals escalation and requires attention. Use: Monitor for worsening as a trigger for action. Example: “My headache is worsening: it started mild but has become severe over the past three days.”

professional help — Assistance from a qualified professional (doctor, physical therapist, mental health professional, dietitian) for health issues that exceed self-care capacity. Use: Define professional help as a resource, not a failure. Example: “I will seek professional help for my persistent shoulder pain because self-care has not resolved it.”

Learning, Building, and Transition Infrastructure

learning infrastructure — The body’s capacity to sustain the cognitive demands of learning: attention, memory, processing speed, frustration tolerance, and sleep-dependent consolidation. Learning depends on body state. Use: Assess your learning infrastructure before committing to intense study. Example: “My learning infrastructure is weak this week due to poor sleep; I should review rather than learn new material.”

building infrastructure — The body’s capacity to sustain the prolonged, focused effort required to build complex systems, write books, create products, or execute ambitious projects. Building requires sustained attention, emotional stability, and stress tolerance. Use: Maintain building infrastructure through consistent recovery. Example: “My building infrastructure is strong because my sleep, nutrition, and recovery routines are stable.”

transition infrastructure — The body’s capacity to sustain the high uncertainty, emotional volatility, and sustained effort of major life transitions (career change, overseas study, relocation, role shift). Transitions deplete capacity rapidly. Use: Protect transition infrastructure as a high-priority asset. Example: “During my career transition, I must prioritize sleep and recovery because my transition infrastructure is under high demand.”

energy runway — The amount of time a person can sustain current activity levels given their current energy reserves and recovery capacity. Energy runway is analogous to fuel range. Use: Calculate your energy runway before committing to long-term projects. Example: “My energy runway is about two weeks at this intensity; I need to build in recovery days to extend it.”

stress tolerance — The amount of stress a person can handle before function degrades. Stress tolerance is not fixed; it varies with sleep, recovery, nutrition, and overall capacity. Use: Monitor stress tolerance as a capacity indicator. Example: “My stress tolerance is low this week; small frustrations feel overwhelming.”

recovery capacity — The body’s ability to restore function after load. Recovery capacity is determined by sleep quality, nutrition, movement, and nervous system regulation ability. Use: Build recovery capacity alongside work capacity. Example: “My recovery capacity is high because I sleep well, eat consistently, and practice regulation daily.”

Body-Health Loop Concepts

action — Any behavior performed in response to or in service of health, capacity, or goals. Action is what the person actually does, drawing on available capacity. Use: Align action with current capacity, not with ambition alone. Example: “My action today will be focused on essential tasks only, given my low energy.”

feedback — The body’s response to action. Feedback can be immediate (sensation change after movement) or delayed (improved sleep quality after a week of consistent routine). Feedback closes the body-health loop. Use: Attend to feedback to guide the next adjustment. Example: “The feedback after my walk is that my back tension decreased and my energy increased.”

adjustment — The modification of behavior based on feedback. Adjustment is the learning mechanism of the body-health loop. Each cycle should produce better awareness, more accurate interpretation, more effective regulation, deeper recovery, and more sustainable action. Use: Make small adjustments and observe the effect. Example: “My adjustment is to take a five-minute break every hour, based on feedback that continuous work produces tension.”
