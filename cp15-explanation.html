16  Explanation

When facts, structure, and causes are organized, they become an explanation.

A thing may be described accurately and still remain unexplained. A result may be named, a sequence may be shown, and even a cause may be mentioned, yet the reader may still not feel that understanding has fully arrived. Why? Because explanation is not just the presence of information. It is the organization of information into an intelligible account. Facts must be arranged. Causes must be connected. Conditions must be shown in their proper place. The parts must hold together.

This is why explanation matters.

Explanation is one of the central powers of structured expression. It turns observation into understanding. It moves language from showing that something happened to showing why it happened, how it happened, and what relations make the outcome intelligible. A strong explanation gives the mind not just pieces, but a shape it can follow.

16.1 What is explanation?

At the level of ordinary language, an explanation is an organized account that makes a fact, event, process, or condition more understandable.

We notice explanation through simple patterns such as:

  • connecting facts and causes

  • showing how parts fit together

  • distinguishing shallow and deep explanation

  • making the whole account coherent

Examples include:

  • explaining why a project failed

  • explaining how a plant grows

  • explaining why a person became tired

  • explaining why one method works better than another

Explanation does not merely add more sentences.
It arranges meaning into a form that answers why or how.

16.2 Why explanation matters

Explanation matters because description alone does not satisfy thought.

We need to know:

  • what makes an explanation clear

  • how explanation differs from mere description

  • why some explanations remain shallow

  • what gives an explanation coherence

Without explanation, language may still report, describe, classify, and compare, but it does not yet make the phenomenon intelligible as a whole. Explanation is where structured expression becomes truly powerful.

16.3 Key distinctions

16.3.1 1. What makes an explanation clear

A clear explanation does not simply mention relevant elements. It arranges them so that the reader can see the logic of the account.

Feature Description Example
clear object the reader knows what is being explained why the machine failed
relevant causes or conditions the main explanatory factors are visible weak maintenance, overload, heat
ordered structure the parts are arranged in a followable way first cause, then consequence, then result
sufficient connection the explanation shows how the parts relate overload caused heat, heat damaged the system

Examples:

  • The machine failed because it was overloaded, and the overload caused heat to build up in a poorly ventilated system.

  • The child became tired because the walk was long, the weather was hot, and there had been too little rest earlier.

This matters because clarity in explanation depends not only on true content, but on well-ordered content.

16.3.2 2. Shallow vs. deep explanation

Not all explanations go equally far.

Type Description Example
shallow explanation gives a surface reason but little structure The project failed because it was difficult.
deeper explanation identifies more specific causes, conditions, or relations The project failed because goals were unclear, coordination was weak, and time was insufficient.

Examples:

  • Shallow: He is tired because he worked.

  • Deeper: He is tired because he worked for many hours without enough rest, and the heat increased the strain.

  • Shallow: The plan failed because it was bad.

  • Deeper: The plan failed because it depended on unrealistic timing, lacked clear responsibility, and broke down under changing conditions.

This matters because strong explanation usually goes beyond first appearance and identifies the structure beneath the result.

16.3.3 3. Explanation vs. mere description

Description tells what something is like. Explanation tells why or how it is so.

Mere description Explanation
The room is cold and dark. The room is cold because the window is open and little sunlight enters.
The system is slow. The system is slow because each request passes through too many steps.
The child is quiet. The child is quiet because he is tired after the trip.

Examples:

  • The road is wet describes a condition.

  • The road is wet because it rained all night explains it.

  • The report is unclear describes a quality.

  • The report is unclear because its main claim is never defined explains it.

This matters because expression becomes more powerful when it can move from visible surface to intelligible cause or structure.

16.3.4 4. Explanatory coherence

An explanation is stronger when its parts fit together without contradiction or disorder.

Feature Description Examples
coherent explanation the causes, conditions, and results support one unified account a clear path from poor planning to delay
incoherent explanation parts are disconnected, inconsistent, or poorly arranged mixed causes, unrelated details, missing links

Examples:

  • The meeting became unproductive because the goal was unclear, the discussion lost focus, and no one organized the next step.
    This is coherent because the parts support one line of explanation.

  • The meeting failed because the room was small, the weather was cold, one person wore red shoes, and the printer was old.
    This may be incoherent if the details do not contribute to the central explanation.

This matters because an explanation is not just a group of possible reasons. It is an organized account whose parts hold together.

16.4 Core patterns

This chapter rests on a few basic patterns.

16.4.1 Basic expressive patterns

  • Explanation organizes facts, causes, and conditions.

  • A clear explanation shows what is being explained.

  • Some explanations are shallow and some are deeper.

  • Explanation differs from mere description.

  • A strong explanation has coherence.

  • Understanding grows when parts are connected into one intelligible account.

16.4.2 Basic language patterns

  • ___ happened because ___.

  • The explanation is that ___.

  • This can be explained by ___.

  • A shallow explanation would be ___.

  • A deeper explanation would include ___.

  • This is description, not yet explanation.

  • The explanation is coherent because ___.

16.5 Explanation and the movement of this volume

Explanation gathers together the major resources developed in the previous chapters.

16.5.1 Earlier chapters supplied the building blocks

  • statement established the claim

  • description showed the phenomenon

  • definition stabilized terms

  • structure arranged parts

  • sequence showed order

  • process showed unfolding

  • cause and effect showed production

  • condition and constraint showed dependence and limitation

16.5.2 Explanation now composes them

  • it brings facts, structure, and causal relations into one account

  • it turns organized material into intelligible understanding

This is a central step in structured expression. Language is no longer only showing pieces of reality. It is making their connection meaningful.

16.6 Examples

Here are some clean examples of explanation in use.

16.6.1 Simple explanation

  • The room is cold because the window is open.

  • He is tired because he slept too little.

  • The plant is weak because it did not get enough water.

  • The machine stopped because the power was cut.

16.6.2 More developed explanation

  • The project slowed because the goals were unclear, which created repeated confusion and wasted time.

  • The child became sick because the weather was cold, the clothes were too thin, and there was not enough rest.

  • The report is hard to follow because its main term is never defined and its points are not ordered clearly.

16.6.3 Shallow vs. deep

  • Shallow: The process failed because it was inefficient.

  • Deeper: The process failed because it required too many repeated steps, lacked coordination, and created delays at each stage.

16.6.4 Coherent explanation

  • The system became unstable because demand increased, the design was not built for that load, and the resulting delays caused further errors.

  • Recovery was slow because the body lacked rest, movement remained limited, and progress could not build steadily.

These examples show that explanatory language often includes:

  • reason

  • structure

  • causal relation

  • depth

  • coherence

16.7 Why this matters

To understand explanation is to understand one of the highest practical forms of structured expression.

Without explanation, language may still present many true observations, but they remain only partly useful. A person may know what happened and still not understand it. Explanation gives the missing organization. It shows how facts, causes, and conditions belong together.

Explanation is also essential because many later structures depend on it:

  • reasoning becomes stronger when explanation is clear

  • evaluation improves when the basis of judgment is explained

  • whole thought becomes more persuasive and stable when explanation is coherent

  • larger forms of writing depend on explanation to connect parts into meaning

  • structured expression reaches one of its central powers when it can explain rather than merely report

Structured expression therefore requires more than accurate language. It requires intelligible organization.

16.8 Chapter summary

This chapter introduces explanation as the organized account that makes a fact, event, or condition more understandable.

When facts, structure, and reasons are arranged coherently, they form an explanation. Important ideas include what makes an explanation clear, shallow versus deep explanation, explanation versus mere description, and explanatory coherence.

The central lessons of this chapter are:

  • explanation organizes facts, structure, and causes

  • clarity depends on ordered and relevant connection

  • some explanations are shallow and some are deeper

  • explanation differs from mere description

  • explanatory coherence matters

  • understanding grows when parts are made intelligible together

16.8.1 Core Knowledge (Must Remember)

Core Idea Key Insight Must Remember
Explanation An organized account that makes a fact or event more understandable Turns observation into understanding — the central power of structured expression
Clarity in explanation Clear object + relevant causes + ordered structure + sufficient connection Clarity depends on well-ordered content, not just true content
Shallow vs. deep Shallow gives surface reason; deep identifies specific structure “It failed because it was difficult” (shallow) vs. “Goals were unclear, coordination weak” (deep)
Explanation vs. description Description tells what; explanation tells why or how “The road is wet” (description) vs. “It rained all night” (explanation)
Coherence Parts fit together without contradiction or disorder An explanation is not a list of possible reasons — it is an organized account
The goal of explanation To make the phenomenon intelligible as a whole, not just to add more sentences Facts + structure + causal relation = explanation

To recognize explanation is to strengthen one of the central powers of language: the power not only to show what is there, but to make clear why it is so.
